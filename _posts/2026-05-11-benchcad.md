## 中文

AI for hardware 这个方向上最基础的一件事，是让模型从一段意图描述出发生成基本的几何结构——一个齿轮、一个支架、一个法兰。

工业 CAD 这个题听起来很适合 RLVR。它几乎是一个自我封闭的系统：参数化建模的操作集合有限（sketch、extrude、revolve、sweep、loft、fillet、chamfer……），每个 part 都可以用代码精确表达，render、voxel IoU、几何检查全部能 100% 自动化 verify。比起 math reasoning 那种 reward 容易被 hack 的领域，CAD 的 reward 是干干净净的"这块铁是不是符合规格"。

但等我真的开始做才发现，远看是个干净问题，进去之后每一层都很糙。

### 第一层：商业大模型

GPT、Claude、Gemini 在通用 VQA 上很强，但放到工业 CAD 这个语境里，VLM 和 text 的对齐糟糕到出乎意料。模型能分得清"这是个圆柱"，但分不清"这是 5 mm 还是 50 mm"。给一个齿轮，能数出有齿，但齿轮模数、压力角、节圆直径这些信息，哪怕在图里以数字标注，它也接不住几何意义上的含义——比例、尺寸、相对位置在工业里就是规格本身，模型当成了"附在图旁边的文字"。

你不能让它去看图、估出"哦这两个孔的中心距大概是法兰外径的 0.6 倍"。它要么完全无视尺寸文字、要么记住数字但不知道这个数字在三维空间里占多少 ratio。这跟它在 chartQA 或 OCR 上的表现是两件事。

### 第二层：自己训小模型

如果你想绕过 frontier model 自己从头训一个，会撞到一个更深的问题：CAD 里相当多的几何关系是要算的，不是看出来的。

一个 spur gear 的 outside diameter 是 `(Z+2)·m`。一个公制螺纹的螺距按 ISO 标准查表给出。一个圆锥滚子轴承的滚子半角是按预定接触角推导出来的。这种"engineer 通过公式得到答案"的关系，小模型基本学不到——它只能 see-what + imitate，看到圆就画圆，看到齿就画齿，没有"公式"这个抽象层。你给它 10000 个齿轮训练样本，它能学会"齿轮长什么样"，学不会"齿数和直径的代数关系"。

这是 small model 路线最深的瓶颈。Frontier 模型有这个抽象的能力（推理出来 outside_diameter = (Z+2)·m 不难），但它的 vision–text 对齐又不足以执行；小模型 vision 没问题，但没有公式抽象层。两端都有 gap。

### 所以我做了个 benchmark

要量化"现在的模型到底差在哪、差多少"，市面上没有合适的工具。CAD 评测圈子里有几个工作，但要么集中在 sketch 层、要么集中在 mesh，没有 cover 我关心的"从图 / 描述生成 buildable 的工业零件代码"这件事。所以我从平时跑 VLM 的日常观察出发，把每天踩到的 failure mode 整理成 task，做了 BenchCAD。

它要回答两个问题。第一个：目前最强的模型在普通工程师每天做的任务上能做到什么水平？第二个：能不能通过有限数据的训练让模型获得这个能力？跑下来——前者比预期差很多，后者基本是否定的，没有泛化能力。

### 数据和任务

数据是 17000+ 条 execution-verified 的 CadQuery 程序，106 个工业零件家族——齿轮、弹簧、钻头、各种支架、叶轮、风罩、螺纹件、轴承零件。每个程序都跑得通、都 render 过。

四个 task：

- **VQA**：看渲染图回答关于零件的问题（几何属性、尺寸关系）
- **code QA**：看代码描述零件
- **image → code**：看零件写程序
- **code edit**：看代码 + 一个改动要求，改三行让它满足新约束

### 打分公式

全文在 `bench/SCORING.md`：

```
score = 0.60·iou + 0.20·essential_pass + 0.10·feature_f1 + 0.05·cd + 0.05·hd
```

- `iou` 是 voxel IoU @ 64³，fixed orientation 下算。我们不用 rotation-invariant IoU 做总分——摆放对方向也是任务的一部分，让模型靠 24 rotation 试一遍蒙到分是奖励运气不是奖励建模。`iou_rot24` 作为 diagnostic 列单独 report。
- `essential_pass` 是 per-family 手写的 op 检查：`sweep+helix` 对 `torsion_spring` 是 essential，用 `circle+extrude` 凑出来不算。这一项专门为了治"圆柱伪装成弹簧"的 hack——我们让 GPT-5.4 生成一个 torsion spring，silhouette 看着对，render 过了 exec 过了，剖开一看 helix 整个被它用平拉的圆柱糊过去。一根铁柱子，套了一层弹簧的外皮。
- `feature_f1` 是 `{chamfer, fillet, hole}` 三个 indicator 的 F1。
- `cd` 和 `hd` 是 Chamfer 和 Hausdorff，权重小是因为跟 IoU 相关性太高，留着主要为了在 IoU 0.9+ 区间还能拉开档次。

13 个家族（chair、table 这种几何上没有 canonical 关键 op 的）在 `essential_pass` 上标 N/A。N/A 时把 0.20 这项拿掉，剩下的 ×1.25 重归一化，使 N/A 样本既不被罚也不被加分。

### 跑下来三件值得说的事

外形几乎所有模型都能做对——silhouette 对，从远处看像那么回事。但只要把视角换到剖面或者俯视，结构就崩：螺纹不见、fillet 全是 0、draft angle 全没了。模型擅长猜零件的"外壳"，但工程上能用的细节几乎全在内部。这跟第一层观察"VLM 不懂尺寸"是一脉相承的：模型在 visual 上摸得到的是 outline 不是 geometry。

CAD 工作的日常其实是 edit 而不是 from scratch。没几个工程师真坐下来从空白文件写一个 part，都是改三行已有程序。我们的 code edit 任务是四个 task 里最难的，比 image → code 难得多——而这也是 production 真正用得到的形态。LLM 在 CAD 这个领域的实际 gap，最大就在这块。

家族之间不迁移。在 gears 上 fine-tune 对 gears 有用，对 springs 帮助小到反直觉。我以前以为 gears 和 splines 之间应该共享一些 helix / pitch 的几何先验，模型并不这么聚类。它学的是 surface-level 模式，不是工程意义上"相似零件"的内在结构。这是"小模型学不到公式"的另一个面：没有"机械零件"的抽象，只有"长这样的物体"的抽象。

详细 leaderboard 和提交入口在 [project page](https://benchcad.github.io/BenchCAD_webpage/)。Paper：[arXiv:2605.10865](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10865)。

## English

The most basic thing AI-for-hardware needs is a model that takes an intent description and produces the underlying geometric structure — a gear, a bracket, a flange.

Industrial CAD sounds like a clean RLVR target. It's almost a self-contained system: the parametric op set is finite (sketch, extrude, revolve, sweep, loft, fillet, chamfer…), every part can be expressed exactly as code, and render / voxel IoU / geometric checks can be verified 100% automatically. Compared to a domain like math reasoning where rewards are hackable, the CAD reward is the cleanest "is this piece of metal to spec."

Then I actually started working on it and discovered the problem is clean from far away and rough in every layer up close.

### Layer one: frontier VLMs

GPT, Claude, Gemini are strong at general VQA, but in the industrial CAD context their vision–text alignment is much worse than expected. The model can tell "this is a cylinder," it cannot tell "this is 5 mm or 50 mm." Show it a gear: it can count teeth, but module, pressure angle, pitch circle diameter — even labeled numerically in the image — don't connect to any geometric meaning. Proportions and dimensions, the spec itself in industrial work, get treated as text floating next to the picture.

You cannot ask one to look at an image and estimate "the center-to-center distance between these two holes is about 0.6× the flange OD." It either ignores the dimensional text entirely, or it memorizes the numbers without knowing what ratio they occupy in 3D space. This is a different failure mode from anything you'd see on chartQA or OCR.

### Layer two: training your own small model

If you bypass frontier models and try to train your own, a deeper problem shows up: a significant fraction of CAD geometric relations are computed, not seen.

A spur gear's outside diameter is `(Z+2)·m`. Metric thread pitch is given by an ISO table. A tapered roller bearing's half-angle is derived from the design contact angle. The "engineer computes the answer from a formula" relation isn't accessible to a small model — it can only see-and-imitate. Sees a circle, draws a circle; sees teeth, draws teeth; no formula layer. Show it 10,000 gear training examples and it learns what a gear looks like, not the algebraic relation between tooth count and diameter.

That's the deepest bottleneck on the small-model path. Frontier models have the abstraction (deriving `OD = (Z+2)·m` is not hard for them), but their vision–text alignment is too weak to execute. Small models have the vision, but no formula layer. Both ends are gapped.

### So I built a benchmark

To quantify where exactly current models are failing and by how much, nothing in the existing CAD evaluation literature covered what I cared about. There are sketch-level benchmarks and mesh benchmarks, none specifically asking "can you generate buildable industrial part code from a description or image." So I assembled BenchCAD by turning the failure modes I'd run into daily into tasks.

It tries to answer two questions. First, what's the ceiling of current frontier models on tasks an engineer routinely does? Second, can limited fine-tuning data give the model that capability? On the first answer: much worse than I expected. On the second: essentially no — the generalization isn't there.

### Data and tasks

The dataset is 17,000+ execution-verified CadQuery programs across 106 industrial families — gears, springs, drills, brackets, impellers, fan shrouds, threaded fasteners, bearing components. Every program runs. Every part has been rendered.

Four tasks:

- **VQA** — ask questions about a rendered part (geometric properties, dimensional relations).
- **code QA** — describe the part from the program.
- **image → code** — produce the program from the rendered part.
- **code edit** — modify an existing program to satisfy a new constraint.

### Scoring

Full spec in `bench/SCORING.md`:

```
score = 0.60·iou + 0.20·essential_pass + 0.10·feature_f1 + 0.05·cd + 0.05·hd
```

- `iou` is voxel IoU at 64³ in fixed orientation. We don't use rotation-invariant IoU in the headline number — getting orientation right is part of the task, and crediting 24 trial rotations is crediting luck. `iou_rot24` is reported separately as a diagnostic.
- `essential_pass` is a hand-written per-family op check — `sweep+helix` is essential for `torsion_spring`, substitutes don't count. That term exists specifically to kill the cylinder-pretending-to-be-a-spring hack. We asked GPT-5.4 for a torsion spring; the silhouette looked right, the code executed, the render passed. Open the part up and there was no helix anywhere — a plain circle + extrude under a spring-shaped skin. A solid rod wearing a spring costume.
- `feature_f1` is the F1 over `{chamfer, fillet, hole}` indicators.
- `cd` and `hd` (Chamfer and Hausdorff) have small weights because they correlate heavily with IoU; they're there to break ties at the 0.9+ end.

Thirteen families (chair, table, where there's no canonical defining op) are N/A on `essential_pass`. When that happens we drop the 0.20 term and rescale the remaining 0.80 by ×1.25 — N/A samples are neither penalized nor rewarded.

### Three observations worth pulling out

Almost every model gets the silhouette right. From a distance the parts look like the parts. Cross-section or top view tells a different story — threads vanish, fillets read as 0, draft angles round to nothing. Models are good at guessing the outer shape of a part. Most of what makes a part actually buildable lives inside. This is the same observation as "VLMs don't understand dimensions" from layer one — models see outlines, not geometry.

The day-to-day of CAD work is editing, not authoring. No engineer sits down and writes a part from scratch — they open an existing program and change three lines. Our code edit task turned out to be the hardest of the four, harder than image → code by a clear margin. It's also the task closest to how CAD is actually used. The biggest LLM gap in this domain sits right here.

Families don't transfer the way intuition suggests. Fine-tuning on gears helps with gears. It helps surprisingly little with springs. I would have guessed gears and splines share something at the helix/pitch level, but the model doesn't cluster them that way — it learns the surface, not the engineering taxonomy. This is the other face of "small models can't learn formulas": there's no abstraction "mechanical part" inside the model, only "thing that looks like this."
